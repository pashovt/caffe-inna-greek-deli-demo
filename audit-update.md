## The Gap

While Caffe-inna has strong coverage across third-party platforms, they **do not control** the central brand experience or customer journey:

1. **No owned website** — customers discover via Google, social, or delivery platforms first.
2. **No central hub** — each platform (Just Eat, Uber Eats, Instagram, TikTok, Facebook) operates independently; no single source of truth for hours, menu, events, or story.
3. **No email capture** — no owned audience for updates, seasonal offers, event announcements, or community building.
4. **No analytics control** — cannot see full customer journey from discovery → order/visit decision.

## Opportunity: Controlled Website as Hub

A website would **not replace** Just Eat/Uber Eats/social channels — it would **centralise** them:

- **Consolidate ordering**: Link to Just Eat + Uber Eats from one place, plus direct phone CTA for call-ahead collection.
- **Own the brand story**: Share the authentic Greek cafe narrative, owner story, and "why we exist" content that third-party platforms can't deliver.
- **Capture email/SMS**: Build owned audience for events, seasonal menu updates, community news (e.g., Greek festival specials, holiday hours).
- **Analytics insight**: See where customers come from, what they care about, where they drop off (Microsoft Clarity heatmaps/session recordings).
- **Control hours/menu/proof**: Update once, not across 5 platforms. Avoid conflicting hours/pricing info.

## Real Customer Journey Today

Current path likely looks like:
1. Customer searches "Greek food Long Eaton" or "cafe near me" on Google.
2. Finds Google Business Profile (5-star rating draws them in).
3. Clicks through to **either**:
   - Just Eat or Uber Eats to order delivery/collection
   - Instagram/TikTok to see real food visuals
   - Phone number to call directly
4. **No website option** — so no central place to see full story + menu + order options + events in one place.

With a website, customer can:
1. Search "Caffe-inna Long Eaton" → land on website.
2. See brand story, real food photos, full menu with prices, customer reviews, and clear CTAs.
3. Choose their path: order on Just Eat, order on Uber Eats, call ahead, or visit in-person.
4. Sign up for email updates if they want (owned audience).

## Recommendations

1. **Build controlled website** — use as hub linking to existing ordering platforms + social channels.
2. **Add Microsoft Clarity** — free heatmap/session recording to see how customers interact with menu/CTA buttons.
3. **Add email capture** — simple form for "get updates on new menu items, events, Greek specials" (low-friction, value-driven).
4. **Centralise proof** — pull Google reviews, Instagram content, TikTok clips into website to reinforce trust without forcing customers to visit 5 platforms.
5. **Track lead sources** — UTM parameters on social bio links → website to see which channels drive most orders/calls.
6. **Future: direct online ordering** — long-term option to own ordering experience (no 15-30% commission to Just Eat/Uber Eats), but not urgent; start with hub + link strategy.

## Platform Commission Context

Third-party ordering platforms typically charge **15-30% commission per order**. For a £9.30 gyros wrap:
- Just Eat/Uber Eats commission: ~£1.40–£2.79 per order
- Over 100 orders/month: £140–£279 in commission costs

A website with direct ordering option (future phase) could reduce this leak, but immediate value is **brand control + audience ownership + centralised discovery**.

---

**Status**: Caffe-inna is **not invisible** online — they have active ordering + social presence. But they **do not control** the customer journey, brand narrative, or owned audience. A website fixes that gap without replacing what's working.
